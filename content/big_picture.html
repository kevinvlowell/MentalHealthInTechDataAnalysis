Who’s working in tech?

According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, there are an estimated 12.4 million workers in information technology in the U.S. The industry is lucrative, and expanding. Technology companies account for 10% of the nation’s economy. The output of tech companies has greatly changed the ways in which we communicate, create, share, and play, in ways particularly palpable and beneficial during the covid-19 pandemic, which has digitized our once physical school, work, and social lives.

 

 

But who’s actually working in tech? Are perceptions of tech as a young and male dominated endeavor true? And what is landscape of physical and mental health in these tech workers? We look into some of these answers, using data collected in the 2016 and 2018 Open Sourcing Mental Illness Mental Health in Tech Surveys.

Is there diversity in tech?

Although many more workers responded to the survey in 2018 than 2016, the pattern of gender breakdowns in both years is similar: tech workers are predominantly male.

 

When we take in addition the consideration of age, we see most tech workers, across genders and across survey years, are relatively young, between 25-40 years old.

 

Why are there so few women in tech? Identifying and remedying dropout points, whether in encouraging early school STEM education in women and nonbinary individuals, in encouraging these underrepresented groups to study computer science in college and beyond, and encouraging workspace diversity and especially diversity in senior leadership, may help close the gender gap in tech.

Mental health in tech workers

We assess the prevalence of mental health disorders in tech workers. Of the respondents, 441 (23.8%) report having an ongoing mental health disorder, whereas many more individuals 949 (50.8%) report having had a mental health disorder in the past. This may be due to selection bias, in which individuals with acute, ongoing mental health disorders would less likely be reponsive to such a survey or currently employed in the tech industry.

 

These estimates of mental health prevalence in tech workers are higher than global estimates, in which the prevalence of ongoing mental health disorders of any kind is projected to be around one in ten people (10.7%), or 792 million individuals worldwide. As stated by the American Psychiatric Association, stigma against mental health, at the institutional and individaully internalized levels, are very much prevalent concerns, and so surveys of these kinds may underestimate the true number of individuals struggling.

Stigma and mental health

In addition, one of the topics that we wanted to explore in depth was the stigma surrounding mental health in the tech workplace. How strongly stigmatized or not stigmatized is it? Has stigma evolved over time as the topic of mental health has garnered more attention? Does it vary between demographic groups? In order to uncover potential answers to these interesting questions, we sought to generate some quantitative, overall measure of stigma for each survey participant: we called this metric the “stigma score.” To generate a stigma score for each participant, we quantified the responses to several questions whose responses were indicative of stigma, and summed the scores of each of these questions together to get an overall stigma score. The higher the numerical score, the greater we deemed the stigma surrounding mental health was for that individual. As an example, the survey question ““Would you feel comfortable discussing a mental health issue with your coworkers?” allowed survey participants to choose from the responses “Yes,” “Maybe,” and “No.” Each of these responses was recoded to values of 1, 2, and 3, respectively.
